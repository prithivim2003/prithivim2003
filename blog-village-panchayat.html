Self Governed Villages for Well Governed India

A study on fiscal health of village panchayats and its impacts on grass root governance

article.render // complete

Author

M Prithiviraj

Affiliation

Rising Leaders 2026 Cohort

Category

Indian Politics · Governance

Introduction

Mahatma Gandhi once propounded that the soul of India lives in its villages. After 76 years of independence, around 63 percent — nearly 900 million Indian citizens — continue to live in villages. Villages host 70 percent of the nation's entire workforce. Yet how these villages are governed, who governs them, and where the fiscal thrust for village governance comes from reflects an alternate and troubling reality.

Civilisationally, India's core governance was decentralised — each village had its own sources of revenue creation and administered itself accordingly. The colonial impact shifted decision-making power from villages to distant headquarters. Today, villages are governed and funded from metropolitan centres. The 73rd Constitutional Amendment Act envisioned democratising grass root governance by making villages self-governing entities — but it gave only political authority. Panchayats have lost their process knowledge on generating their own revenue and remain dependent on other arms of government for funding. The aim of this study is to explore how villages can become fiscally independent and operationally self-sustaining.

How Are the Villages Funded?

The 73rd Constitutional Amendment Act constitutionalised village panchayats as self-governing bodies and created a list of avenues through which villages can generate their own revenues. Article 243H of the Indian Constitution empowers village panchayats to create their own sources of revenue for administration. Articles 243G and 243I mandate state and central governments to devolve funds to village panchayats for their functioning. Constitutionally, all three tiers of government bear responsibility for the financial governance of villages. However, real numbers reveal stark inequality.

The Finance Commission is responsible for setting the fund share and managing devolution. The existing model has three critical vulnerabilities:

1. 85 percent of funds come from Union tax devolution under Finance Commission mandates, 10 percent from state governments, and only 5 percent from the village's own revenue sources.

2. The nature of grants: the majority of funds are tied grants, usable only on projects established by the Union and state governments — leaving no room for village panchayats to make independent spending decisions.

3. Village panchayats are chronically underfunded. According to the 16th Finance Commission, each village receives a minimum of ₹3 lakh as a central grant — a significant portion of which must be spent on centrally specified projects.

Problem Statement

Villages, though politically empowered by the 73rd Amendment, remain financially dependent on Union and state governments. This dependency prevents panchayats from becoming genuinely self-governing bodies. Despite constitutional provisions to increase own-source revenues, these account for only 5 percent of total village revenues — creating a wide gap in standards of living between villages and metropolitan areas.

Objectives of the Study

This study aims to: understand citizen satisfaction with service delivery under village panchayats; assess the efficiency of panchayats in grass root governance; evaluate citizen awareness of village financial governance; gauge villagers' willingness to participate in revenue-generating activities; and explore the feasibility of an effective Own Source of Revenue model for village panchayats.

Methodology

The study employs a mixed-methods approach combining quantitative and qualitative design. Data was collected through structured interviews comprising both closed-ended questions with rating scales and open-ended questions designed to elicit feedback and suggestions. The sample size is 20 respondents — all natively born and raised in Indian villages — drawn from 6 Indian states, with horizontal and vertical diversity across gender and age groups (23–45 years).

Key Findings

The following emerged from the survey:

Only 41% of respondents participated in their last Gram Sabha meeting. 70% said their village has an operational waste management system, while only 40% reported an efficient sewage management system. 88% confirmed functional tap water connections — the highest-scoring indicator, reflecting the structured impact of the Jal Jeevan Mission.

On service quality, 75% rated Anganwadi services 3–4 out of 5, and 65% gave similar ratings to roads and street lighting. On satisfaction with services relative to taxes paid, responses were mixed: 40% rated 4–5, 40% rated 2–3, and 10% gave the lowest score of 1.

On the question of who bears responsibility for village governance, 52% attributed responsibility to the state government, 23.5% to the village panchayat, and 23% to the Union government. On willingness to participate in community revenue-generating initiatives, 41% said yes, 41% said it depends on the scheme, and only 17% were unwilling.

The most cited ideas for community-led revenue generation were: animal husbandry, community-owned agro-production, renewable energy, tourism, and water conservation. Around 30% of respondents felt that better utilisation of existing funds — avoiding misexpenditure, corruption, and unnecessary infrastructure — would itself be sufficient.

Analysis

Infrastructure-intensive projects involving construction are generally well implemented, while operations-intensive services such as sanitation and sewage maintenance remain weak. The high score for tap water availability reflects the demonstrable impact of centrally structured schemes like Jal Jeevan Mission — but this also underscores the dependency on Union-driven funding rather than panchayat-led initiative.

Gram Sabha participation at only 41% indicates that grass root democratisation still has significant ground to cover. The fact that a majority of respondents attribute governance responsibility to the state rather than to their own panchayat reflects a governance perception gap — citizens are yet to internalise the panchayat as their primary governance institution. Encouragingly, over 80% of respondents expressed willingness or conditional willingness to participate in community revenue-generating activities, signalling strong latent civic potential in villages.

Citizenry Suggestions

Agriculture-led community initiatives were the top choice among respondents — from value-added agro-based products to self-help group-based agricultural enterprises. Approximately 30% cited renewable energy as a viable revenue model. Successful precedents exist: Odanthurai village in Tamil Nadu and Hiware Bazar in Maharashtra have both adopted renewable energy as their primary own-source revenue model, offering replicable templates for the rest of the country.

Reflections

After engaging directly with villagers on fiscal governance and fund-sharing models, it is clear that Indian villages hold enormous untapped potential. They are endowed with human capital, natural resources, social cohesion, and a sense of community responsibility — all powerful foundations for self-sustaining governance. The problem is structural: villages rely almost entirely on top-down funding, reducing panchayats to bureaucratic arms of higher government rather than genuine institutions of self-governance.

Gram Swaraj cannot fulfil its promise of true swaraj until villages are financially empowered to generate and independently utilise their own resources. Scaling village-led initiatives would also contribute directly to reducing the agrarian economy's declining share in GDP and addressing the mounting employment crisis in rural India.

Conclusion and Recommendations

The study concludes with the following roadmap for achieving full Gram Swaraj:

On fund devolution: Increase fund devolution under Finance Commission recommendations for Panchayati Raj Institutions. Increase the proportion of untied grants, giving villages greater freedom over expenditure decisions. Create a consultation mechanism between panchayats and state governments for villages to request plan-specific grants — mirroring the relationship that exists between states and the Union.

On own-source revenue generation: A nationwide campaign by the Ministry of Panchayati Raj to promote village-led revenue initiatives, with reward mechanisms for successful panchayat administrations. Linking the One District One Product initiative with village SHG clusters, enabling each village to participate in producing a designated product marketised through a national e-commerce platform. Nationalised banks and the RBI to adopt venture capital-style models — loaning villages against their own creative initiatives and taking equities. NITI Aayog to develop replicable templates and SOPs across sectors — especially agriculture and renewable energy — for panchayats to implement without requiring independent policy design capacity. Merit-based capacity development for educated women in villages by the Ministry of Skill Development, to encourage women-led production initiatives. Employment pipelines connecting SME loan projects from young entrepreneurs with village manpower and resources — with village panchayats holding equities in such projects.
