China's Semiconductor Self-Reliance

Strategic Targets vs. Technical Realities

article.render // complete

Author

M Prithiviraj

Category

International Politics

Topic

Technology & Security · China Studies

Introduction

Semiconductors are the new oil of the 21st century, argues Chris Miller, the author of Chip War. AI ambitions, defence automation, and computing capacities all boil down to one piece of hardware — the semiconductor chip. Security studies scholars argue that the ability to produce and control the semiconductor supply chain will confer greater leverage in the 21st-century battle among the big powers. China, a traditional engineering state, wants to seize this momentum and lead the chip race. China's ambition is to become completely self-reliant in advanced semiconductor manufacturing technology.

China's Ambitions

In 2015, soon after Xi took over the Politburo, he launched the 'Made in China 2025' programme — a 10-year strategic initiative to make China the leader of technological innovation in the world. Out of the 10 sectors mapped in the policy, semiconductor self-reliance was one of the key targets. It aimed to achieve 70 percent self-reliance in semiconductor technology and manufacturing by 2025. While chip production is a complicated process with multiple factors and technologies involved, China had aspired to take control of the entire production pipeline.

In 2015, China was the largest global consumer of semiconductors — accounting for up to 60 percent of consumption — but producing only 15 percent of global output. They identified a strategic gap that could be leveraged against them. The leading producers of semiconductors were Taiwan, the Republic of Korea, and Japan. China had strained relationships with all these nations and was therefore cautious about its dependencies on them. The United States was also a technology leader, holding 55 percent of the entire intellectual property involved in advanced semiconductor manufacturing. China wanted to change this by taking greater control of the full range of technologies involved in chip manufacturing — free from reliance on any Western nation. However, China has reached only around 50 percent of its aimed targets as of 2025.

Domestic Pursuit

In order to accelerate the race, China launched a series of 'big fund' programmes in which the state would fund domestic companies willing to invest in chip manufacturing. Three rounds of big funds have been created to fuel this momentum. In 2024 came 'Big Fund 3.0' — the largest ever state-funded technology programme — with 344 billion yuan (approximately 47.5 billion US dollars) to be invested over the next 15 years for acquiring the latest technologies in semiconductor manufacturing. The Central Science and Technology Commission (CSTC), established under the control of the Chinese Communist Party, was created to oversee these efforts. These investments are channelled into state-owned entities such as SMIC, YMTC, and Hua Hong, whose task is to build completely indigenous 2nm and 3nm node chips — the kind heavily used in GPUs and AI chips. China also leverages its dominant critical minerals industry to support its manufacturing ambitions.

Strengths

China's primary strength lies in its established manufacturing base, accounting for 23 percent of global semiconductor production and a dominant 38 percent share in the testing and packaging segment. China is home to more than 250,000 IC chip design engineers, alongside a larger pool of cost-competitive labour that drives production at scale. Chinese investments in R&D have yielded strong dividends — including success in reverse-engineering tools and equipment required in manufacturing. China is also the producer of 80 percent of the world's silicon wafers, the foundational raw material in chip making. With sufficient funds and natural resources, China has seen a tremendous increase in its production capacities, closing the gap between its aspirational targets and current realities.

Vulnerabilities

Despite this momentum, there are critical strategic vulnerabilities in China's chip ambitions. EUV lithography machines — built exclusively by ASML of the Netherlands — are the only devices capable of printing circuits smaller than 7nm. While China holds a significant share of production in the 25nm-and-above categories of chips used in everyday electronic devices, smaller node chips — especially those at 8nm or below — are crucial for AI computation. China remains reliant on DUV-based lithography devices and has not yet achieved access to EUV technology. While the West dominates the design and architecture layer of the chip supply chain, and East Asia dominates manufacturing and packaging, China continues to face significant vulnerabilities in chip design, architecture, and EDA software — areas where it remains highly dependent on the United States.

US Control and the Chip War

The most significant obstacle to China's semiconductor self-reliance is the United States. The US identified China's technological rise as a direct challenge to its technological hegemony and moved swiftly to constrain it. A series of legislative and regulatory instruments followed — including the CHIPS and Science Act of 2022 and the expansion of the Foreign Direct Product Rule (FDPR) — which banned Chinese companies from acquiring technologies essential to advanced chip manufacturing. The US also forced chipmakers such as Nvidia, Samsung, and AMD to stop selling advanced chips and GPUs to Chinese companies. Simultaneously, the US is investing heavily in domestic chip manufacturing, countering China's Big Fund 3.0 with its own 57 billion US dollar commitment to domestic semiconductor production. China has been increasingly vocal in its opposition to these sanctions, framing them as technological aggression.

Conclusion

China recognised the strategic importance of semiconductors early and has laid out a clear roadmap, acquiring substantial process knowledge in the chip manufacturing space. It has demonstrated real capability — producing 25nm-grade chips at mass scale sufficient to supply its domestic market. Its cost-competitive labour and favourable industrial conditions continue to make it a leading destination for testing and packaging. The real challenge lies with chips smaller than 8nm nodes, which require high-end inputs where the United States retains decisive control. This remains the most significant pain point in China's semiconductor ambitions.

Yet Chinese engineers are relentlessly working to close this gap. Huawei has successfully built its 5nm node Kirin 9030 chip, which is now set to be mass-produced at SMIC foundries. Reuters has reported that engineers in Shenzhen have successfully built a laboratory prototype of an indigenous EUV lithography machine — and if this becomes commercially viable, China will be very close to matching the United States in the chip war. While it may appear that China is far from its targets and unlikely to achieve full self-reliance by 2030, the trajectory is clear: with the same momentum, intensive upskilling, and relentless upscaling, China is closing the gap day by day.
