MA Dissertation · April 2025

Quad and the Indo-Pacific Security Architecture: Institutionalization and its Impacts

Central University of Kerala · Department of International Relations and Politics · Supervised by Dr. Reinhart Philip

This dissertation examines the feasibility and implications of institutionalizing the Quadrilateral Security Dialogue (Quad) — the partnership of Australia, India, Japan, and the United States — and its potential impact on the Indo-Pacific security architecture. The study analyses each member state's strategic calculus regarding institutionalization, maps the progressive security evolution of Quad from a humanitarian coalition in 2004 to a mini-lateral with growing strategic imperatives, and argues that while formal institutionalization in the NATO mould is unlikely, a cooperative security governance model — driven by shared democratic values, working groups, and the annual leadership summit mechanism — is already emerging organically. The dissertation uses qualitative research methods, including policy document analysis, joint statement review, and secondary literature to answer whether Quad's institutionalization will strengthen the Indo-Pacific security framework, and whether it serves the interest of all four member states.

Research Paper · 2024

The Great Nicobar and Agalega Islands: Fortifying Indian Ocean with Strategic Infrastructures

Department of International Relations and Politics · Central University of Kerala

This paper examines two landmark infrastructure projects — the NITI Aayog's ₹72,000 crore Great Nicobar Island development project, and India's construction of an airstrip and jetty at Agalega Island, Mauritius — as strategic tools to fortify India's dominance in the Indian Ocean. The paper argues that these investments represent a decisive shift in India's national security doctrine towards an ocean-centric approach. The Great Nicobar project, located near the Strait of Malacca, would give India transshipment control and dual-use military capability at one of the world's most critical chokepoints. The Agalega Island airstrip, jointly inaugurated by Prime Minister Modi in February 2024, positions India to build a networked maritime surveillance architecture from the Western Indian Ocean, countering China's growing naval presence and its strategic foothold in Djibouti.

Research Paper · 2024

Thiruvalluvar's Diplomacy and Statecraft: Epitome of Bharath's Knowledge and Wisdom as Vishwa-Mitra

Department of International Relations and Politics · Central University of Kerala

This paper argues that the 5th-century Tamil saint Thiruvalluvar's Thirukkural — a collection of 1,330 couplets spanning ethics, governance, and statecraft — constitutes one of the earliest and most comprehensive treatises on the theory and practice of international relations. Drawing on chapters concerning the duties of a king, the excellence of an army, the character of an envoy, and the nature of friendship between states, the paper demonstrates that Thirukkural's prescriptions remain of deep contemporary relevance to India's foreign policy posture as a Vishwa-Mitra. The paper contends that India's values-based multilateralism, its civilizational commitment to dharma, and its "security through development" approach in the Indo-Pacific all find their philosophical foundations in Thiruvalluvar's vision of righteous statecraft.

Research Paper · 2025

Civilian Conflicts of Maritime South Asia: A Need for Dynamic Outlook of Maritime Security of Indian Ocean

Department of International Relations and Politics · Central University of Kerala

This paper examines the proliferation of non-traditional, civilian-centric maritime security conflicts in South Asia — including the India-Sri Lanka fishermen dispute (with over 535 Indian fishers arrested in 2024 alone), the India-Pakistan fisher exchange mechanism, the India-Bangladesh maritime boundary frictions, regional migration crises involving Rohingya populations, and the Indian Ocean's role as a conduit for narcotics trafficking via the "Golden Crescent" and "Golden Triangle" routes. The paper argues that the failure of SAARC and the absence of a South Asian equivalent to ASEAN's Fisheries Consultative Forum have left the Indian Ocean dangerously under-governed at the civilian level. It calls for issue-specific, people-centric regional mechanisms modelled on the ASEAN way, which can complement India's naval and coast guard capabilities in ensuring a safe, open, and equitable Indian Ocean.

Research Statement

Intellectual Agenda

My scholarship is driven by a single animating question: how can India best leverage its civilizational heritage, geographic position, and democratic values to build a free, open, and rules-based order in the Indo-Pacific? Whether examining the Quad's institutionalization prospects, India's strategic infrastructure in the Indian Ocean, the ancient wisdom of Thiruvalluvar, or the ungoverned civilian conflicts of South Asian waters, my work consistently returns to the challenge of translating India's growing strategic ambition into durable, legitimate, and inclusive regional governance. My doctoral research will deepen this inquiry, engaging with the emerging literature on mini-lateralism, cooperative security governance, and India's shift from strategic autonomy to multi-alignment as the defining framework of its twenty-first-century foreign policy.
